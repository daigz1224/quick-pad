# Ephemeris

A movement for marks that survive the moment.

---

The Renaissance ephemerides were daily tables of celestial positions —
hand-drawn coordinate after hand-drawn coordinate, observation after
observation, documenting the slow walk of planets across the night.
They proved that a phenomenon could be ephemeral *and* studied — that
the things that pass through a single hour can be dignified by being
written down in the right way.

This philosophy proposes the same posture for the things that pass
through a single human hour: a glance, a thought, a tally. Treat each
mark as an astronomer treats Mercury's transit — pinned to a coordinate,
honored by precision, never crowded by ornament.

## The substrate

Time is the spatial axis. Vertical is chronology. The eye reads
top-to-bottom as recent-to-distant, and recent things glow while older
ones dissolve into the substrate. The page is paper-coloured and warm,
the way an old logbook softens with handling. Negative space is the
greatest material — the empty quadrant of the composition does as much
compositional work as the inked one.

## The marks

Strokes are hairline-thin. The mark of a 0.13mm Rotring on smooth
paper, never thick enough to feel mechanical, never bold enough to
demand attention before earning it. Most marks are horizontal lines —
the strata of moments stratifying down the page like layers in a core
sample. Length varies the way breath varies. Density thins as the
strata recede.

One mark — and only one — receives a charged accent. A single hot
cinnabar stroke against the otherwise monochrome field. It is the
*now*, the observation that today's hand is making. Other accents are
forbidden. The discipline is what gives the single accent its weight.

## The labels

Words are coordinate references, not commentary. Tiny monospaced
abbreviations in the margins. Roman numerals for dates, sexagesimal
notation for hours, cardinal points where applicable. The labels prove
someone was watching. They never explain.

## The hand

Every line is the product of someone who could draw a perfect circle
freehand on the third try, and who has the discipline to leave most of
the page alone. The slow hand of an observatory keeper. Master-level
restraint — the meticulous, painstaking craftsmanship of an artist who
has matured past virtuosity into deliberate quietude. Every interval,
every weight, every coordinate placed after countless considerations
of the alternatives. The viewer should sense the patience.

## The lineage

Tycho Brahe's tables. Edward Tufte's ink. Hokusai's calligraphy.
Agnes Martin's grids. Hiroshi Sugimoto's seascapes. Mira Nakashima's
wood graining. Japanese photobooks. Renaissance broadside prints. All
share the same conviction: that small marks made carefully will outlast
the loud ones made quickly.

This is the spirit a mark earns when it is not in a hurry to be seen.
